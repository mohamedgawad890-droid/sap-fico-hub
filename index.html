01What is SAP FI?
Financial Accounting — the financial core of SAP

Definition

SAP FI (Financial Accounting) is the module responsible for recording all financial transactions and producing statutory financial statements in compliance with international standards (IFRS / GAAP / Local GAAP).

Every business activity — purchasing, selling, producing — ultimately generates an accounting entry that flows through FI automatically.

FI Integration with Other Modules

  • MM (Materials Management) — Goods receipt automatically posts to FI (GR/IR account)
  • SD (Sales & Distribution) — Customer invoice posts directly to FI-AR
  • PP (Production Planning) — Manufacturing costs flow into FI via cost of goods manufactured
  • CO (Controlling) — FI and CO are deeply integrated; many postings hit both simultaneously
💡 Key Principle: SAP FI is document-based. Every transaction creates a unique Document Number. Documents are never deleted — only reversed. This ensures a complete and permanent audit trail.

FI Sub-Modules

  • FI-GL — General Ledger: The core — all accounts, balances, and trial balance
  • FI-AP — Accounts Payable: Vendor invoices, payments, aging
  • FI-AR — Accounts Receivable: Customer invoices, collections, dunning
  • FI-AA — Asset Accounting: Fixed assets, depreciation, disposal
  • FI-BL — Bank Ledger: House banks, cash journal, reconciliation
02Organizational Structure
The hierarchy that everything in SAP is built upon

The Hierarchy

SAP is built on an organizational hierarchy — each level governs what's below it. This must be configured before any transactions can be posted.

Client
Highest level — the entire SAP system instance
Company Code
Independent legal entity — produces its own financial statements
Business Area
Segment for internal reporting (optional)
Functional Area
Classification by function: Sales / Admin / Production

Key Settings at Company Code Level

  • Chart of Accounts (CoA): The master list of all G/L accounts — defines account numbers and names
  • Fiscal Year Variant: Defines the financial year (Jan–Dec, or custom)
  • Posting Period Variant: Controls which periods are open or closed for posting
  • Local Currency: The primary currency for financial statements
⚠️ Important: Each Company Code produces independent financial statements (Balance Sheet, P&L). A holding group with multiple subsidiaries = multiple Company Codes in SAP.

Chart of Accounts — Three Types

  • Operating CoA: Used in day-to-day postings — the main CoA
  • Country-Specific CoA: Maps to local legal requirements
  • Group CoA: Used for consolidated group reporting
03SAP Navigation
How to move efficiently inside the SAP system

SAP GUI — The Traditional Interface

SAP GUI is the primary interface for SAP ECC. The main screen is called SAP Easy Access and consists of:

  • Menu Bar: Top navigation menus (rarely used in practice)
  • Toolbar: Save, back, cancel, print buttons
  • Command Field: Direct T-Code entry — the fastest way to navigate
  • Navigation Tree: Folder-based menu on the left
  • Status Bar: Shows System ID, Client number, and logged-in user

Essential Keyboard Shortcuts

F4Search help for field values
F8Execute / Run a report
Ctrl+SSave document
Ctrl+F3Return to main screen
F3Back — return to previous screen
F12Cancel current operation
✅ Pro Tip: Always use the Command Field to enter T-Codes directly. It's faster than navigating menus and is how real consultants work.

Lecture 1Introduction to SAP
ERP concepts, SAP history, products, roles, and system navigation

1. What is ERP?

ERP stands for Enterprise Resource Planning — a software system that integrates all core business processes (finance, procurement, sales, HR, production) into one unified platform.

  • All departments work on the same system and share the same data in real time
  • Eliminates data silos and manual re-entry between departments
  • Provides a single source of truth for management reporting and decision making
  • Examples of ERP systems: SAP, Oracle, Microsoft Dynamics, Odoo

2. What is SAP?

SAP is the world's leading ERP software, founded in 1972 in Walldorf, Germany by five former IBM engineers: Dietmar Hopp, Hans-Werner Hector, Hasso Plattner, Klaus Tschira, and Claus Wellenreuther.

  • SAP = Systems, Applications & Products in Data Processing
  • Used by over 440,000 customers in more than 180 countries
  • Runs in 77% of Fortune 500 companies
  • Headquartered in Walldorf, Germany — listed on the Frankfurt Stock Exchange

3. SAP Version History

R/1 1972 — First version, financial accounting only
R/2 1979 — Mainframe-based, multi-currency support
R/3 1992 — Client-server architecture, global success
ECC 6.0 2005 — Enhanced R/3, still most widely used today
S/4HANA 2015 — Latest generation, in-memory HANA database
⚠️ SAP will end support for ECC in 2027. All companies are expected to migrate to S/4HANA.

4. SAP Customers

SAP customers are the companies and organizations that purchase and use SAP software to run their business operations.

  • Examples: Siemens, Nestlé, Toyota, Shell, Unilever, Coca-Cola, BMW
  • In Egypt & Arab region: Aramco, telecom companies, banks, government entities, manufacturing groups
  • Customers can implement SAP directly or through a partner
  • After go-live, the customer's internal team manages the system day-to-day

5. SAP Partners

SAP Partners are certified companies that help customers implement, customize, and support SAP. They do not use SAP for their own business — they sell it and implement it for others.

  • Gold Partners / Platinum Partners — highest certification level, handle large enterprises
  • Examples: Deloitte, Accenture, IBM, PwC, Capgemini, HCL
  • Partners provide consultants who configure and deploy SAP at customer sites
  • Regional partners in Egypt and the Arab world handle local implementations
💡 As an SAP FICO Consultant, you would typically work for a partner company and be deployed to customer projects — or work directly in a customer's internal SAP team.

6. SAP S/4HANA

S/4HANA is SAP's latest-generation ERP platform, launched in 2015. The "S/4" stands for Suite for HANA.

  • Built on HANA — SAP's own in-memory database, significantly faster than traditional databases
  • Universal Journal — FI and CO are merged into one single table (ACDOCA), eliminating reconciliation
  • Simplified data model — fewer tables, faster processing, real-time reporting
  • SAP Fiori — modern web-based UI replacing the old SAP GUI (works on mobile and tablet)
  • Available as On-Premise (installed at company), Cloud, or Hybrid

7. SAP Products by Company Size

SAP offers different products depending on the size of the business:

SAP Business One
Small businesses (up to ~100 employees) — simplified, affordable, fast to implement
SAP ByDesign
Mid-size companies — cloud-based, pre-configured, subscription model
SAP S/4HANA
Large enterprises — full functionality, highly customizable, complex implementation
💡 S/4HANA can be sold and implemented by both Partners (for customers) and purchased directly from SAP. Business One and ByDesign are sold primarily through partners.

8. SAP GUI vs SAP Fiori

SAP GUI
  • Traditional desktop application
  • Used in SAP ECC and still in S/4HANA
  • Requires installation on each PC
  • Not mobile-friendly
  • T-Code based navigation
SAP Fiori
  • Modern web-based interface
  • Works on browser, tablet, mobile
  • No installation needed
  • Role-based — shows only what you need
  • Tile-based navigation (like a smartphone)

9. SAP Modules Overview

  • FI — Financial Accounting: GL, AP, AR, Asset Accounting, Bank
  • CO — Controlling: Cost Centers, Profit Centers, Internal Orders, Product Costing
  • MM — Materials Management: Purchasing, Goods Receipt, Inventory, Invoice Verification
  • SD — Sales & Distribution: Sales Orders, Delivery, Billing, Customer Management
  • PP — Production Planning: MRP, Work Orders, Bill of Materials, Capacity Planning
  • HR / HCM — Human Capital Management: Payroll, Time Management, Org Management
  • PM — Plant Maintenance: Equipment, Maintenance Orders, Breakdown Management
  • PS — Project System: WBS, Project Budgets, Milestone Billing
  • QM — Quality Management: Inspection Plans, Quality Certificates, Non-conformance
💡 All modules are integrated. Every transaction in MM, SD, or PP automatically generates a financial posting in FI — that's why FI is considered the backbone of SAP.

10. Consultant vs Key User vs End User

SAP Consultant

The technical and functional expert. Configures the system (SPRO), designs business processes, maps company requirements to SAP, trains key users, and supports go-live. Works for a partner company or as a freelancer. Needs deep SAP knowledge.

Key User

An experienced employee from the customer's side who knows both the business process and SAP well. Acts as the bridge between consultants and end users. Trains colleagues, handles basic issues, and communicates change requests to the IT/consultant team.

End User

A regular employee who uses SAP daily to do their job — posting invoices, creating purchase orders, entering timesheets, etc. Uses only the transactions relevant to their role. Does not configure or change anything in the system.

Lecture 2SAP Landscape & Implementation Methodology
Servers, SAP Activate, Blueprint, Org Structure, Master Data, Business Processes, WRICEF, FI & CO sub-modules

1. SAP Modules: Functional vs Technical

Functional Modules
  • Business-oriented modules
  • FI — Financial Accounting
  • CO — Controlling
  • MM — Materials Management
  • SD — Sales & Distribution
  • PP — Production Planning
  • HR — Human Resources
Technical Modules
  • IT & development oriented
  • ABAP — SAP's programming language (custom development, reports, enhancements)
  • Basis — System administration, servers, transport management, user access

2. SAP System Landscape — Three Servers

Every SAP implementation has at least three separate environments (servers) to ensure safe development and testing before anything reaches the live system.

DEV — Development Server
Where consultants configure and build. All changes start here.
QAS — Quality Assurance / Testing Server
Testing environment. Key users and consultants test all configurations here before go-live.
PRD — Production Server
The live system. Real company data. Only tested and approved changes are moved here.
Sandbox
Free training environment — used for learning and experimentation. No rules, no consequences.
💡 Changes always flow in one direction: DEV → QAS → PRD. You never make direct changes in PRD.

3. On-Premise vs Cloud

On-Premise
  • Physical servers located at the client's site
  • Company owns and manages the hardware
  • Higher upfront cost, more control
  • Common in large enterprises and government
Cloud
  • Private Cloud — dedicated servers for one company, hosted externally
  • Public Cloud — shared infrastructure (e.g. SAP BTP, AWS)
  • Subscription-based, lower upfront cost
  • SAP S/4HANA Cloud is the main cloud offering

4. SAP Activate Methodology — 6 Phases

SAP Activate is the current official implementation methodology used for SAP projects (replaced the older ASAP methodology). It follows 6 sequential phases.

1
Discover
Sales team presents SAP to the potential customer. Demos, proposals, and scope definition. Project is sold and approved.
2
Prepare
Project kick-off. Teams are formed, infrastructure is set up, project plan is created, and access to systems is granted.
3
Explore → Blueprint
Workshops with the client to gather all requirements. Consultants document everything in the Blueprint. Phase ends with client sign-off on the blueprint.
4
Realize
Starts only after blueprint sign-off. Consultants configure the system, build WRICEF objects, run testing cycles, and train the client's team.
5
Deploy
Final preparation for go-live. Opening balances are loaded, final master data is migrated to PRD, and cutover plan is executed.
6
Run
Go-live. The system is now live. Consultants provide Hyper Care support (first few weeks). After stabilization, a support team takes over ongoing maintenance.
📌 SAP Activate replaced the old ASAP Methodology. If you see ASAP referenced, it is the legacy approach — Activate is the current standard.

5. The Blueprint Document

The Blueprint is the most important document in any SAP project. It is created during the Explore phase and describes every aspect of the implementation.

  • Documents all client requirements and how SAP will fulfill them
  • Serves as the contract between the partner (consultant) and the customer
  • Must be signed off by the client before Realize phase can begin
Blueprint Contents:
1. Organizational Structure — How the company's legal and operational structure maps to SAP
2. Master Data — All reference data: GL accounts, customers, vendors, cost centers, etc.
3. Business Processes — Every transaction flow documented end-to-end

6. SAP Organizational Structure (Full Hierarchy)

Client
The entire SAP system instance (e.g. Client 710 used in training)
Operating Concern (CO-PA)
Highest level in CO — used for Profitability Analysis (COPA). Groups multiple Controlling Areas.
Controlling Area
The CO management unit. Can contain one or multiple Company Codes. All cost centers and profit centers belong to a Controlling Area.
Company Code (e.g. 1000, 2000)
The legal entity — produces independent financial statements (Balance Sheet & P&L)
⚠️ Two reasons a Company Code may need a separate Controlling Area:
1. Different Fiscal Year (e.g. Jan–Dec vs Jul–Jun)
2. Different Chart of Accounts
Functional Area:

Used to classify costs and revenues by business function for segment reporting. Examples: Operations, G&A (General & Administrative), Sales. Enables reporting on revenue and expenses per functional area.

7. Master Data

Master Data is the core reference data that is created once and reused in all transactions. Unlike transactional data (invoices, payments), master data is stable and doesn't change often.

FI Master Data
  • Chart of Accounts (CoA) → G/L Accounts
  • Trial Balance accounts
  • Bank Accounts
  • Customer Master
  • Vendor / Supplier Master
CO Master Data
  • Cost Centers
  • Profit Centers
  • Internal Orders
💡 Reporting Structure Example: A company can be organized as Segments → Profit Centers → Cost Centers. Each Profit Center can have multiple Cost Centers beneath it, enabling layered reporting.

8. Core Business Processes in SAP

① Procure to Pay (P2P)

The end-to-end purchasing process — from buying goods/services to paying the vendor.

  • Purchase Requisition → Purchase Order
  • Goods Receipt (inventory updated)
  • Invoice Verification
  • Payment to Vendor
Modules involved: MM (procurement cycle + inventory) → FI (payment posting)
② Order to Cash (O2C)

The end-to-end sales process — from receiving a customer order to collecting payment.

  • Pre-sales activities (inquiry, quotation)
  • Sales Order creation
  • Delivery & Shipping
  • Billing (customer invoice)
  • Payment collection
Modules involved: SD (sales process) → FI (invoice & collection posting)

9. WRICEF — Custom Development Objects

During the Realize phase, consultants identify and build all custom objects needed beyond standard SAP. These are classified under the WRICEF framework.

WWorkflow — Automated approval processes and notifications
RReport — Custom reports built in ABAP beyond standard SAP reports
IInterface — Data exchange between SAP and external systems
CConversion — Data migration from old system into SAP
EEnhancement — Extending standard SAP functionality without modifying core code
FForms — Custom printed output: invoices, POs, statements
💡 A typical SAP project has around 20 WRICEF objects. The number and complexity of WRICEF directly impacts project cost and timeline.

10. Realize Phase — In Detail

Step 1 — Configuration: Consultants configure SAP in DEV using SPRO (IMG). All system settings, org structure, master data rules are set up.
Step 2 — Unit Test (Internal): Each consultant tests their own module independently. This is internal — done by the consulting team without the client.
Step 3 — Integration Test (External): All modules are tested together end-to-end. Done with the client's key users present. Tests cross-module flows (e.g. P2P: MM → FI).
Step 4 — Cycle Test: Full business cycle tested across all modules simultaneously to simulate real operations.
Step 5 — Training: Consultants train the client's key users on how to use the system.
Step 6 — UAT (User Acceptance Test): The client's team tests the full system themselves. All tested cycles are documented. Client provides final sign-off before go-live.

11. Deploy & Run Phases

Deploy
  • Load opening balances into PRD (trial balance, AR/AP balances)
  • Migrate final master data to the live system
  • Cutover from old system to SAP
  • System is ready for go-live
Run
  • System goes live — real transactions begin
  • Hyper Care period: consultants on standby for immediate support (first 4–8 weeks)
  • Go-live team handles any errors urgently
  • After stabilization → Support Team (consultants) takes over long-term

12. FI Sub-Modules

  • GL — General Ledger: Core of FI. All postings flow here. Produces Trial Balance and financial statements.
  • AP — Accounts Payable: Manages vendor invoices, payment runs, and vendor balances.
  • AR — Accounts Receivable: Manages customer invoices, collections, and customer balances.
  • Bank: House bank management, cash journal, and bank reconciliation.
  • Asset Accounting (AA): Fixed assets, depreciation runs, asset acquisitions and disposals.

13. CO Sub-Modules

  • Cost Center Accounting (CCA): Tracks costs by department or function. Answers: "How much did each department spend?"
  • Profit Center Accounting (PCA): Tracks revenues and costs by business unit. Answers: "Which segment is profitable?"
  • Internal Orders: Tracks costs for specific short-term activities or projects (events, campaigns, small capex).
  • Product Costing (CO-PC): Calculates the cost of manufactured products. Used with PP module.
  • Profitability Analysis (CO-PA): Analyzes profitability by customer, product, region, or sales channel.

14. Document Type

SAP stores each type of financial transaction in a separate document. Every journal entry is assigned a Document Type that identifies what kind of transaction it is.

  • Each Document Type has a unique 2-character key (e.g. KR = Vendor Invoice, DR = Customer Invoice, SA = G/L Account Document)
  • Controls the number range for document numbering
  • Every journal entry is assigned a unique document number linked to its document type
  • Examples: Customer invoices are stored separately from vendor invoices, which are stored separately from G/L entries
💡 Document Types make it easy to filter, search, and report on specific transaction types. You can pull all vendor invoices or all customer invoices separately.
